Methodology

1

We collected aerial LiDAR imagery to obtain detailed roof features across Philadelphia

2

Imagery was processed to see how much solar radiation hits each part of rooftops, taking shadows from other buildings and land features into account

3

Solar radiation data was summarized for each building

4

A conservative estimate of actual harvestable solar energy was calculated based on the current limitations of solar panels

Detailed Methodology:

 

The City of Philadelphia purchased LiDAR (Light Detectection and Ranging) imagery captured from planes in April of 2015 (available from PASDA).  LiDAR  measures distance by emitting pulsed laser light and measuring the reflected pulses.  These pulses reflect off of the Earth's surface and features on it, such as buildings and vegetation.  By comparing the emitted light to the reflected light, an accurate height of ground surface and other features can be calculated.  LiDAR imagery was captured before trees grew leaves again after the winter, without snow on the ground, and when water levels were normal.  This high-resolution elevation data is an important initial step for determining the solar potential of rooftops across the city.

 

ESRI's solutions for local government includes a model for calculating solar radiation that we used to process LiDAR data to quantify solar radiation on rooftops across Philadelphia.  This was performed by modeling the average path of the sun for each month of the year to determine the amount of solar energy that reaches the ground surface and other features.  This calculation takes into account how features on the land block solar radiation and create shadows, which would reduce the efficiency of solar panels.  LiDAR imagery tiles were processed across the entire city, covering about 196 square miles.  After combining monthly solar radiation data into a yearly average and converting units to kwH/m2/day, the resulting solar radiation tiles were stitched into a single raster and clipped to building footprints.  A cutoff of 3 kwh was applied to the solar irradiance data and summed for each building.  A conservative estimate of solar potential of 20% of total solar irradiance to account for the efficiency of solar panels.
